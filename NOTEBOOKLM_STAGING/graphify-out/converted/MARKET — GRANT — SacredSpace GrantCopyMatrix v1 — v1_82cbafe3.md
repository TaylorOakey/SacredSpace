S∆CR3DSP∆CE: Neural Forest is an immersive, AI-integrated ecological environment and community learning platform located in Northampton County, NC. The project constructs a living sanctuary space where ecological design, adaptive artificial intelligence, and STEAM education converge into a single, accessible public installation.
The problem it addresses is structural: rural communities in historically underserved regions are increasingly excluded from both ecological connection and meaningful AI literacy. Standard digital education programs feel culturally alien to land-based communities. Environmental programs ignore the digital economy. The Neural Forest dissolves this binary by teaching AI systems thinking through the language of ecology — network architecture understood through root systems, data sovereignty framed through land stewardship, adaptive AI experienced through living light and sound.
The installation operates simultaneously as a physical ecological environment and a live, open-source AI operating system. Visitor presence triggers sensor inputs; the system queries a semantic knowledge graph (ChromaDB vector database); local AI inference (Ollama, running on-device) generates adaptive environmental responses — changes in light, sound, and atmosphere — that are archived as Memory Motes in a 13-table SQLite database. The feedback loop runs continuously. The environment learns from every interaction.
This is not a passive exhibit. It is a functional Living Field Simulator: a closed-loop system that ingests community input, updates its internal model, and modifies its outputs without human intervention between cycles — while remaining fully transparent, open-source, and replicable by any community with modest hardware.

FUNDER TAGS:  NEA, NEH, NSF, USDA, IMLS, Kresge, Mellon


The primary community served is the rural population of Northampton County, NC and the broader Roanoke-Chowan region — a historically Black, predominantly rural area with limited access to both ecological public spaces and technology education infrastructure. The county's median household income falls significantly below state and national averages, and residents have documented limited access to broadband, maker spaces, and STEAM programming.
Secondary communities served include K–12 students, post-secondary learners, and adult community members across a 120-mile regional network that spans the NC Triangle (Raleigh, Durham, Chapel Hill), Rocky Mount, Greenville NC, and Hampton Roads Virginia. A documented 79-business regional partner network has been mapped and assessed for alignment with the project's nine-pillar community architecture.
The need is threefold. First, ecological disconnection: rural communities with land-based heritage are losing active relationship with their natural environments. Second, technology exclusion: AI literacy programs designed in tech hubs do not translate to rural cultural contexts. Third, infrastructure gap: there is no existing regional model for nature-grounded, sovereignty-preserving, community-owned digital education infrastructure.
The Neural Forest addresses all three simultaneously — using ecology as the curriculum delivery system, local-sovereign open-source technology as the infrastructure, and the physical sanctuary as the gathering place.

FUNDER TAGS:  USDA Rural Development, NEA, NEH, IMLS, Kresge, NC Arts Council

S∆CR3DSP∆CE is designed from the ground up to resist the structural failure mode of grant-dependent projects. Three systems guarantee post-grant sustainability:

1. The Commercial Engine. SacredArcana Studios LLC operates a four-circle revenue model generating independent income: free education (trust building) → digital artifacts ($8–$45) → premium memberships and consulting ($88–$333) → high-ticket workshops and commissions ($333+). 2. The Open-Source Replication Model. Because the entire technical stack is free, open-source, and publicly documented on GitHub, the program can be replicated by any community organization with a modest hardware investment. This means the sanctuary network grows through replication rather than through central-organization expansion — each new node is self-sustaining from deployment.
3. The Sovereignty Layer. The knowledge infrastructure — 176-note Obsidian vault, SQLite canonical archive, ChromaDB vector knowledge graph — is stored on sovereign local hardware under a canonical governance protocol. It is explicitly designed to be inheritable across leadership transitions and hardware generations. The Sacred Little Forest land trust, held by the 501(c)(3), ensures the physical anchor of the sanctuary is permanently protected from sale or dissolution.

The Neural Forest is equity infrastructure by design, not by addition. Four structural features constitute its equity architecture:

Geographic equity: The project is explicitly sited in Northampton County, NC — a rural, historically Black, economically underserved community that has been systematically excluded from both technology investment and ecological public space development. The sanctuary is not brought to this community as a benevolent outside program. It grows from the community's land, partnerships, and cultural heritage.
Access equity: Participation in the Neural Forest requires no prior technical knowledge, no device, no subscription, and no fee. The ecological interface is the point of entry. Community members who have never interacted with AI engage meaningfully through the physical environment — light, sound, atmosphere — without needing to understand the underlying system.
Data sovereignty equity: No participant data is transmitted to external servers, used to train proprietary AI models, or sold to third parties. This is a structural guarantee enforced by the open-source local infrastructure — not a policy statement that depends on corporate goodwill. In communities that have historically had their data extracted without consent, this distinction is material.
Cultural equity: The Digital Hybrid Multimedia methodology — human-origin creative work extended through AI tools — treats community members as authors and creators, not users. The curriculum is grounded in ecological and ancestral knowledge systems native to the Southside Virginia and NC Roanoke-Chowan region, not imported from a tech hub.

The Neural Forest introduces three innovations that, to our knowledge, do not exist in combination in any existing public program:

1. The Living Field Simulator architecture. The Neural Forest is a closed-loop AI system that updates its internal model in real time based on community interaction — not a static exhibit with scripted responses. The mycelial knowledge graph (ChromaDB semantic embeddings connected by meaning rather than folder hierarchy) means the environment becomes more resonant with each interaction. This is autonomous, adaptive infrastructure that most institutions are still describing theoretically.
2. The Digital Hybrid Multimedia methodology. The project introduces a documented, teachable creative framework — human-origin artistic work extended and enhanced through AI tools — that positions community members as authors rather than users. This methodology does not exist as a named, replicable curriculum in rural STEAM contexts. It fills a specific gap between 'no AI' and 'AI does it for you.'
3. Open-source sanctuary replication. Because the complete technical stack is publicly documented and runs on commodity hardware with zero API costs, the Neural Forest is the first known sanctuary-scale AI installation explicitly designed for community replication. Any organization with a modestly equipped community space can fork the repository and deploy a node. The innovation is not just what it does — it is that it can be given away without losing value.
