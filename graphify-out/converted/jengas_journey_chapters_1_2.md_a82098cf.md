# JENGA'S JOURNEY
## Season One: The Awakening of the Arcana Adept
Canon Seal: @SOURCE Phase: Nigredo I — Nigredo II



A note to the artist: The Construct Realm lives in right angles, compression, and fluorescent light. The Living Realm breathes. Every panel that crosses from one to the other should feel like a different atmosphere entering the lungs. Jenga's eyes are the story's most important recurring image — they must be drawn with full attention in every close-up. We are going to lose them. The reader should not know this yet. But you should.


# CHAPTER ONE: THE CITY THAT FORGETS

## PAGE 1 — Splash
The city at 2am. Aerial establishing shot. A perfect grid of light — the Construct Realm in its most nakedly geometric form. Streets like circuitry. Buildings like stacked rectangles of function. The sky: orange-pink with light pollution, no stars visible anywhere.

Somewhere in the grid, barely visible from this altitude: a single figure. A wall beside it. A color on the wall that has no business being in this city.

CAPTION: "The City does not dream. It does not rest. It runs its programs through the night."

CAPTION: "Everything has a use. Everything has a price. Everything has a surveillance camera."

CAPTION: "Everything except the thing on the wall at the corner of Marcus and 9th."


## PAGE 2 — 2 Panels
Panel 1: Street level. The wall itself — the side of a municipal utility building, gray concrete, pre-tagged with faded official notices about zoning and ordinances. The mural is already more than halfway finished: forest green, emerald, root-brown, amber. We can't see what it depicts in full — just the scale and the color. It is 15 feet wide, 8 feet tall. The contrast with the surrounding gray is not decorative. It is almost violent. A small generator hums somewhere offscreen. Jenga stands at the center of the work, arm raised, spray can in hand.

CAPTION: "He started with the green. Always the green first."



Panel 2: Close on Jenga's face, three-quarter profile. 14 years old. His expression contains a quality of attention that doesn't quite fit the face of a teenager — focused, slightly wide-eyed, as if listening rather than creating. His eyes are his most specific physical feature: alert, direct, with something in them that looks like recognition. Like the wall is telling him something and he is writing back.

CAPTION: "He always said the image was already there. All he did was find it."


## PAGE 3 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: The mural taking shape. We can now see more of it: trees, but not naturalistic ones — sacred geometry trees, fractal-branching, with roots that form spiral patterns below ground-level and branches above that suggest a network rather than a canopy. The colors are alive against the gray city wall. In the lower right corner, nearly finished: an eye inside a triangle. He paints it without pausing, without looking at his reference. It has appeared without being consciously decided.

CAPTION: "He had been painting this same shape for five years."

CAPTION: "He didn't know why."



Panel 2: Wide shot. The street is empty except for Jenga. The mural glows behind him — the green is almost iridescent in the artificial light. He steps back to look at the whole. His silhouette is small against what he's made.

(No caption. No dialogue. The boy and the thing he made.)



Panel 3: Close on the eye-inside-triangle. Done. Jenga's hand in the foreground, can lowered. Behind it: the sigil, precise and careful, exact in a way that the organic geometry of the rest of the mural is not. It looks older. More deliberate. As if it arrived from somewhere else.

CAPTION: "He didn't put it there consciously."

CAPTION: "He never did."



Panel 4: From the rooftop: flashing red and blue lights entering the frame from both ends of the street simultaneously. Two police vehicles converging on the single figure below. The mural blazes between them — too bright to be accidental, too alive to be orderly. But here we are.

SFX: SIREN WHOOP


## PAGE 4 — 5 Panels
Panel 1: Ground level. An officer approaching. Jenga turns — doesn't run. His hands are still paint-stained: green on his fingers, root-brown at his wrist. He holds the can out to the side, not surrendering exactly, not hiding. Just present with what he's done.

OFFICER: "Hands where I can see them. Drop the can."



Panel 2: Jenga complies. His face is calm with the quality of exhaustion, not defiance. He has been here before. This is choreography he knows. Hands up. Can on the pavement. Step back.

OFFICER 2 (approaching from the opposite side): "This the kid from the Morrison Street thing?"

OFFICER: "Check him. He's got priors."



Panel 3: Jenga looking back once at the mural. The officers are between him and it. Over their shoulders: the sigil. The eye inside the triangle. Looking at him.

JENGA (internal): They'll paint over it by Thursday.



Panel 4: An officer looking at the mural. Something shifts in the expression — not aesthetic appreciation, more like a mild unease. Something about it that requires another look.

OFFICER 2 (quietly, to the other): "What is that even—"

OFFICER: "Doesn't matter. Property damage is property damage."



Panel 5: Handcuffs. Jenga's green-stained wrists together. His face still calm. Behind him: the mural. The eye.

CAPTION: "Citation number four."

CAPTION: "This one was different."


## PAGE 5 — 2 Panels
Panel 1: The courtroom. Stark overhead fluorescents — the specific institutional cruelty of that light. The Judge behind an elevated bench: The Honorable T.M. Briggs, a face that has heard many reasons and found most of them insufficient. Jenga below, smaller than he should be in the space. Beside him: his mother, sitting with the posture of a woman who has been apologizing on behalf of someone she loves for a long time and has almost run out of apologizing. A public defender with a thin folder.

Everything in the room is beige or gray. Right angles throughout. The American flag. A clock. A municipal seal on the wall. The Construct Realm in its most unambiguous form: a machine for judging.

CAPTION: "The Honorable T.M. Briggs had a face like a parking violation."



Panel 2: The Judge looking down at the file. Reading. Not yet looking at Jenga.

JUDGE: "Four citations. Public nuisance. Property damage totaling—" (pause) "—notable, given your age."

JUDGE: "Do you have anything to say for yourself?"


## PAGE 6 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: Jenga's face. He is looking at the Judge, but not the way you are supposed to look at a judge. He is looking at him the way he looks at a wall — like he is seeing something the Judge doesn't know is there.

JENGA: "The mural is still up."



Panel 2: The Judge's expression. A pause.

JUDGE: "I beg your pardon?"

JENGA: "They said they'd paint over it. It's still up. It's been six days."



Panel 3: Jenga's mother reaching over to touch his arm. Not to silence him — more like a reflex, a long habit of trying to pull him back from the edge of whatever comes next.

MOTHER (whispering): "Baby—"



Panel 4: The Judge settling back. Reassessing. Looking at Jenga with something that isn't quite curiosity but isn't entirely dismissal either.

JUDGE: "Your mother has no family in the city who can—" (checks file) "—no. She does not."

(beat)

JUDGE: "Your father's information is listed as unknown."

(Jenga's face doesn't move. But something behind his eyes does. Something small and very fast. Gone before it can be named.)

JUDGE: "There is a grandmother. Maternal. North Carolina."


## PAGE 7 — 3 Panels
Panel 1: The Judge leaning forward.

JUDGE: "Here is what I'm prepared to offer, and I will only offer it once. Ninety days with your grandmother. Agricultural county. No spray cans. No walls. You come back without another citation, I dismiss two of the four. You miss a check-in, come back early, or there's so much as a parking ticket—" (pause) "—you understand the alternative."



Panel 2: Jenga processing this. His eyes are slightly unfocused — not vacancy, but calculation. Or something below calculation. We can't read exactly what he's thinking, only that something is happening.

JUDGE: "Do we have an agreement?"



Panel 3: Close on Jenga's eyes. Some decision being made behind them. Something that reads, to the casual observer, as resignation. But look closer — there is something else there. Something that looks almost like recognition. Like a word he has heard before in a language he forgot he knew.

JENGA: "Yeah."

JUDGE: "Yes, Your Honor."

JENGA: "Yes. Your Honor."

CAPTION: "He didn't know why he said yes that quickly."

CAPTION: "He didn't know why his chest did what it did when she said North Carolina."


## PAGE 8 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: A small apartment. Not destitute, but compressed — every surface carrying the layered texture of a life without enough space. Stacked things. Laundry over chairs. But on the walls: Jenga's work covers almost every surface. Small studies, sketches, photographs of murals. References. The recurring shapes. The apartment is trying to be a forest from the inside.

CAPTION: "He had two bags. One for clothes. One for supplies he didn't know yet he wouldn't need."



Panel 2: His shelf. Books, spray cap collections, a cracked phone charging, receipts. And one empty space — a rectangular absence, the faint outline where something stood for years and is now gone. The surrounding shelf is dusty. The absence is not. Something lived there. It has been removed. We do not explain it. We only see it.

(No caption. Just the absence.)



Panel 3: His mother in the doorway. She is not crying — she is past crying into something quieter. She is looking at him with a specific expression: love and relief mixed in a ratio that only makes sense if you have been worried about someone for a long time and the worry has become its own kind of love.

MOTHER: "She's going to feed you. Real food."

JENGA: "I eat."

MOTHER: "Chips are not a food group."



Panel 4: Jenga zipping his bag. He is looking at the wall — his wall, covered in sketches. His eyes move across it the way they move across a wall he is studying. Reading.

MOTHER: "She's good people, baby. Your grandmother. You don't know her but she knows you."

JENGA (not turning): "How does she know me if I don't know her?"

(His mother doesn't answer. The beat holds. The answer is too large for the space between them, or it is not something words do well, or it is both.)


## PAGE 9 — 2 Panels
Panel 1: Jenga's hand flat against one of his sketches on the wall. The recurring shape. Roots. Spirals. The eye inside the triangle. His palm covers part of it — the way you might hold something you're about to put down.

CAPTION: "He had been painting the same thing since he was nine years old."

CAPTION: "He didn't know what it was."

CAPTION: "He knew it was real."



Panel 2: Final view of the apartment from the doorway. Bags in hand. His mother behind him somewhere. The wall of sketches fills the frame — his visual life, five years of the same language being spoken toward something that hadn't answered yet. The recurring shapes. The forest that keeps trying to grow inside a city apartment.

CAPTION: "He left the sketches on the wall."

CAPTION: "He figured whatever came next, he'd need to know what he was coming from."


## PAGE 10 — Splash
He takes a different route to the bus station. It is early morning — not dawn yet, that hour that belongs to no one. He rounds the corner at Marcus and 9th.

The mural is still there.

Full page. The mural in full for the first time — complete, undisturbed. The sacred geometry trees. The spiral roots extending beyond the wall's edges as if they continue into the concrete itself. The network of branches that looks, from this angle, in this particular light, like something underground made visible. And in the lower right corner: the eye inside the triangle. Watching.

Six days, and it has not been painted over.

It catches the pre-dawn half-light and holds it differently than the surrounding gray.

Jenga stands before it. Small against it.

JENGA (internal): Hey.

CAPTION: "He didn't know who he was saying it to."

CAPTION: "The mural did not answer."

CAPTION: "But it didn't feel like silence."

CAPTION: "It felt like being heard."

# CHAPTER TWO: THE EXILE ROAD

## PAGE 11 — 3 Panels
Panel 1: The bus. Interior, early morning. The light through scratched windows is still the city's light — artificial orange, pollution-warm. Jenga sits in a window seat near the back. Bags in the overhead rack. Headphones on, but no music playing — just the passive armor of it.

CAPTION: "The bus to Northampton County left at 6:14am."

CAPTION: "Seventeen other passengers. None of them looked like they wanted to be going where they were going."



Panel 2: Outside the window: the city moving past. The last of the Construct Realm — overpasses, industrial yards, chain link, graffiti on freeway barriers. Jenga clocks a tag he recognizes. One he doesn't. One that seems to be trying to say something and hasn't found the language yet. Then: the first trees.

CAPTION: "The city doesn't end. It just... thins."

CAPTION: "Like something was pulling the buildings apart and replacing them with distance."



Panel 3: Jenga's eyes. He is watching the window. The reflection of the bus interior overlays on the passing trees outside — his face transparent over the green, superimposed on it.

CAPTION: "He hadn't been outside the city since he was six."

CAPTION: "He didn't remember why they left."

CAPTION: "He didn't remember much about before the apartment."

(The empty shelf. The rectangular absence. We don't name it. We remember it.)


## PAGE 12 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: The landscape changes. The highway runs through real green now — patchy at first, then more deliberate. Fields. A silo. A closed roadside stand. The sky grows wider — more of it visible at once than Jenga has seen in years. The horizon is a different concept out here.

CAPTION: "Everything was the wrong color."

CAPTION: "Green was supposed to come in small quantities. Potted plants. The park on 7th. The mural."

CAPTION: "This much green felt—"

(The sentence doesn't finish. Just the view.)



Panel 2: Something on the window.

A crow.

It has landed on the exterior frame of Jenga's window. The bus is moving at highway speed. The crow is there anyway, gripping the narrow ledge with absolute composure. Its eye — the one facing Jenga — has the black-mirror quality of a lens. It is not looking at the passing landscape. It is looking directly at him.

(No caption. This image does the work alone.)



Panel 3: Jenga removing his headphones. Looking at the crow. He does not look frightened. He looks the way he looks at a wall when something in it is asking him for something — patient, listening, trying to understand the language.

JENGA (internal): Birds don't land on moving buses.



Panel 4: The crow, close. One eye. The quality of that eye: reflective, calm, specific. It rhymes with something — not identical, but in the same family as the eye in the lower right corner of the mural on Marcus and 9th. Not the same symbol. The same frequency.

(No caption.)


## PAGE 13 — 3 Panels
Panel 1: Jenga puts his hand to the glass — not to knock on it, not to wave, more like a reflex of contact. The crow's head tilts: not birdlike, but slow and deliberate, tracking his movement.

JENGA (internal): You're not supposed to be here.

JENGA (internal): But neither am I.



Panel 2: Wide — from outside the bus entirely. The bus moving through open land, the windows small rectangles of interior light. On one window: the faint impression of Jenga's face, and, barely visible, the crow. The landscape around them: green and sky and the long, flat, quiet lines of Eastern North Carolina, which is one of the quieter places on earth.

CAPTION: "The crow rode with him for thirty-seven miles."



Panel 3: The crow departing — not in alarm, not suddenly, but with intention. Lifting from the window ledge without hurry. Jenga watching it go. It doesn't look back, but the departure has the quality of a message delivered, not a flight abandoned.

CAPTION: "Then it left."

CAPTION: "He spent the next hour watching the trees."

CAPTION: "Trying to figure out if the trees were watching back."


## PAGE 14 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: Interior of the bus. The other passengers: sleeping, staring, reading on phones, headphones in. No one is looking out the window. Jenga is the only one receiving whatever the outside is transmitting. The only antenna.

CAPTION: "He pulled out his sketchbook."



Panel 2: His hands. Sketchbook open on his knee, pen moving. He is not drawing the crow. Not drawing the landscape. Drawing the shape — the recurring shape, the one that has been with him since he was nine. Roots. Spirals. The network. The eye.

CAPTION: "He always drew when he didn't know how to think."

CAPTION: "The drawing knew things the thinking didn't."



Panel 3: What he has drawn — the sketchbook page. Almost the same as always. But not quite. The roots go slightly deeper than he usually takes them. And the eye inside the triangle contains, faintly, almost not there — a shadow of a second eye inside the first. An eye within the eye. Like a reflection looking back at itself.

He stares at it.

He doesn't remember drawing the inner eye.

(No caption. Let it exist.)



Panel 4: The bus pulling into a small station — barely a station, more a covered shelter with a gravel lot. A hand-lettered sign: NORTHAMPTON COUNTY. The sky here is enormous: uncompressed, the full width of it visible in every direction. The treeline begins immediately beyond the lot. Everything is quieter than he expected — not silence, but a quality of sound that has space inside it. Sound that breathes.

CAPTION: "The town was smaller than his building."

CAPTION: "The sky was larger than anything."


## PAGE 15 — 5 Panels
Panel 1: Jenga stepping off the bus. Bags. He stands in the gravel lot while other passengers file past him, and he breathes — slowly, without deciding to. The air is different. Not just cleaner. Different in kind. It has something dissolved in it that he doesn't have a word for.

CAPTION: "The air smelled like something he didn't have a word for."

CAPTION: "He would learn the word later."

CAPTION: "It was memory."



Panel 2: The lot. A few parked vehicles. And at the far edge: an old pickup truck, well-maintained, parked apart. And beside it: a woman.

She is not what the word grandmother suggested. He had assembled one from distance and television — something soft, something that smelled like baking. This woman is something else entirely. Upright. Still in a way that is not passive — the stillness of someone who has stopped needing to move to be present. She is looking at him across the gravel with an expression that is not the expression of meeting someone for the first time.

(Their eyes find each other across the distance.)



Panel 3: Jenga walking toward her. She does not move to meet him — she lets him come. There is something in this: she is not anxious for his arrival. She does not need to shorten the distance. She is simply present in the waiting, the way a tree is present.

CAPTION: "She looked at him like she'd been expecting him for a long time and was glad the waiting was finally over."



Panel 4: Close: the Grandmother's face. She is in her late sixties, or seventies — it is difficult to say with precision, as if age has settled into her differently than it settles into most people. Her eyes are the most specific thing about her: deep-set, calm, with a quality of seeing that is the same quality as Jenga's eyes, but older and steadier. She has had decades to practice what he is only beginning.

She is the first thing he has seen outside the city that feels like the right color.



Panel 5: They stand before each other. His bags between them. Behind her: the truck, the road, the treeline, the enormous uncompressed sky. Behind him: the bus pulling out of the lot.

GRANDMOTHER: "You're taller than I thought."

JENGA: "You're—"

(He stops. He doesn't know how to finish the sentence.)

GRANDMOTHER: "Yes." (Quiet acknowledgment. As if she knows exactly what he almost said.)


## PAGE 16 — 3 Panels
Panel 1: She takes one of his bags without asking. Walks to the truck. He follows, slightly off his own rhythm.

JENGA: "You don't have to—"

GRANDMOTHER: "I know."



Panel 2: In the truck. She drives with the ease of ten thousand repetitions of this exact road. He sits with his remaining bag on his lap, watching the trees that now line the road on both sides. Close on both sides.

GRANDMOTHER: "How was the ride?"

JENGA: "Long."

GRANDMOTHER: "Mm."

(A pause. He wants to tell her about the crow. He doesn't know why he wants to tell her. He doesn't tell her.)



Panel 3: The road through the trees — the canopy beginning to close overhead, branches from both sides leaning toward each other. The truck small inside the tunnel of green. This should feel claustrophobic. It does not. It feels like entering something.

CAPTION: "He had the feeling — and he recognized this feeling, he knew it from painting, from those moments before the image arrived—"

CAPTION: "—that something had been waiting for him."

CAPTION: "Not in the way of a trap."

CAPTION: "In the way of a welcome."


## PAGE 17 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: The truck turning off the main road onto a dirt track. At the end of it: the Grandmother's house. A structure that appears to have grown rather than been built — additions and extensions that were not planned but happened over decades. A porch that wraps two sides. A garden that is more accurately called a wild zone, organized by a logic that is not human landscaping. A shed being slowly consumed by climbing plants. Trees close on three sides, leaning slightly inward.

CAPTION: "He had expected a house."

CAPTION: "This was something that lived."



Panel 2: The Grandmother parking and stepping out without looking back — moving directly to the garden, already in her own rhythm, which does not restructure itself for his arrival. He gets his own bag.

GRANDMOTHER (not turning): "There's food inside. Help yourself. I'll be in before dark."



Panel 3: Jenga watching her disappear into the garden. Something about the way she moves through it — the plants seem to register her presence. Nothing photographic. A quality of motion, of the space around her shifting as she enters it.

CAPTION: "She disappeared into the garden like she'd been doing it all her life."

CAPTION: "He supposed she had."



Panel 4: His hand on the front door of the house. Old wood. Warm despite the season in a way that wood shouldn't be. Under his palm — at the threshold of perception, right at the edge — something that might be a vibration. Or might be his own pulse reflected back at him. He cannot tell.

CAPTION: "He stood at the door longer than made sense."

CAPTION: "Like something on the other side was deciding whether to let him in."

(A beat. Then the door opens easily. No drama. Just open.)


## PAGE 18 — 5 Panels
Panel 1: Inside. And here — even within Season One's early visual register — the palette shifts. The interior of this house does not obey the Construct Realm's rules. Things on the walls: drawings old and new, weavings, dried plants, photographs spanning decades mixed without chronology. Jars of things. A library covering one full wall — books mixed with objects, seeds, small stones, things that have no retail category. Everything holds a relationship with everything else. The house is organized by a logic he doesn't know the name of yet. He can feel that the logic is there.

CAPTION: "The house smelled like cedar. And something underneath cedar."



Panel 2: Jenga moving through the rooms slowly, touching nothing. His eyes doing what his eyes do — moving with that specific, listening quality, reading the space the way he reads walls.

CAPTION: "And then he saw it."



Panel 3: On the wall of the main room, behind a simple frame on aged paper, slightly foxed at the edges: a drawing in graphite, worn. The same shape. The roots. The spirals. The network of branches. The eye inside the triangle. Exact. Identical to what he has been making since he was nine years old.

In a house he has never been to.

Drawn before he was born.

(Full panel: the drawing, and Jenga's face reflected faintly in the glass of the frame. Both looking at each other.)

CAPTION: "The same thing he'd been drawing since he was nine."

CAPTION: "The exact same thing."



Panel 4: Close on his eyes. Something happens behind them — not confusion. The inverse of confusion. Recognition too large to process all at once, so his eyes are doing it in sections.

CAPTION: "He didn't know what it meant."

CAPTION: "He knew it meant something."



Panel 5: Night. His room: small, simple, the window open onto the dark. He is lying in bed, sketchbook on his chest, not drawing. Outside: the sound of the forest at night, which is not silence but a different category of sound entirely. Calls. Something that moves through the leaves without wind. A layered, breathing presence.

JENGA (internal): Okay.

JENGA (internal): So something is happening.

CAPTION: "He didn't sleep for a long time."

CAPTION: "But he wasn't afraid."

CAPTION: "He had painted the same thing for five years without knowing why."

CAPTION: "That feeling — of doing something without knowing why — was the most familiar thing about this whole night."


## PAGE 19 — 3 Panels
Panel 1: Before dawn. The Grandmother is already in the garden — she has not yet been to bed, or she woke before the birds, or time moves differently in her relationship to it. Jenga is at his window, watching her from above, unseen. She is talking to the plants. Not that he can hear what she says from here. But the body language is clearly conversation — response and reply, not monologue. She pauses, nods. Something in the garden responds.

(No caption. Only observation.)



Panel 2: The Grandmother stopping. Looking up. Directly at his window. Directly at him — as if she has known for some time that he was there. Their eyes meet across the yard.

She nods. Once. Simple. An equal greeting.



Panel 3: Full width. The Grandmother returning to the plants. The house behind Jenga's window. The treeline beyond the garden. The first light at the horizon — not sunrise yet, but its announcement. In that light, the trees are the same green as the mural on Marcus and 9th.

GRANDMOTHER (calling out, without turning, her voice carrying cleanly in the pre-dawn air): "The forest has been waiting."

CAPTION: "He didn't ask how she knew he was awake."

CAPTION: "He didn't ask how a forest could wait for a person."

CAPTION: "He went back to his room and drew."

CAPTION: "The shape again. The same shape. But this time, for the first time—"

CAPTION: "—he wrote a word underneath it."


## PAGE 20 — Splash
Close on the sketchbook page. Full page.

The familiar shape: roots, spirals, the network, the eye inside the triangle. And underneath it, in Jenga's handwriting:

WHY DO I KNOW THIS.

And beneath that — in the same handwriting, but he does not remember his hand moving to write it:

BECAUSE YOU ALWAYS HAVE.

He stares at the page.

He does not remember writing the second line.

(Full page. The drawing. The two lines. The pen still in his hand at the edge of the panel.)

CAPTION: "He looked at it for a long time."

CAPTION: "Then he closed the book."

CAPTION: "He would open it again in the morning."

CAPTION: "Something told him the answers would still be there."

CAPTION: "Something told him they had always been there."

CAPTION: "He was just now beginning to read."
