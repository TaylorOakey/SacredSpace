# JENGA'S JOURNEY
## Season One: The Awakening of the Arcana Adept
Canon Seal: @SOURCE Phase: Nigredo III Element: Earth



A note to the artist: Chapter 3 is the descent into gravity. Chapters 1 and 2 lived in vertical space — aerial shots, the bus window, the open sky. This chapter is horizontal. Close to the ground. The camera should feel heavier. Soil texture, bark texture, the specific roughness of things that don't get cleaned. Jenga is a teenager who doesn't know where anything is. Draw him slightly wrong for every setting he's in — wrong shoes, wrong posture, wrong-sized movements for the space. The Grandmother is never wrong for any setting. That contrast is the whole chapter.


# CHAPTER THREE: THE GRANDMOTHER'S GATE

The ceiling of the small room: old wood planks, the kind that have been painted many times, each coat slightly different from the one beneath it. One crack runs diagonally from the corner. A moth is resting near the window. Outside: bird calls — not one or two, but layered, a sustained conversation between many birds. Not city sounds. Not any sound his nervous system knows how to categorize yet.

CAPTION: "He woke up at 5:47am."

CAPTION: "He knew this because he checked his phone."

CAPTION: "No signal."



Panel 2: His phone screen. Full bars of "No Service." The wallpaper: a photo of a mural — one of his, the green one on a wall somewhere in the city. He stares at it.

JENGA (internal): Okay.



Panel 3: He lies back. The sounds outside continue. He tries to identify them the way he'd identify sounds in the apartment — cataloguing, orienting. In the city there was always something he recognized. Here: bird, bird, something in a tree, wind, something that might be an animal moving, bird, something he has no name for.

CAPTION: "He could not sleep through sounds he couldn't name."



Panel 4: Wide shot through the window: the garden in the early light. Blue-gray predawn. The Grandmother already in it. Moving between the plants with a small basket. She has been awake longer than him. Possibly she is always awake.

CAPTION: "The light here came from a different direction."

CAPTION: "He didn't know what he meant by that."

CAPTION: "He meant it anyway."


## PAGE 22 — 5 Panels
Panel 1: The kitchen. Jenga stands in the doorway in his socks — city socks, thin, wrong for cold floors. The kitchen is full: jars on every surface, dried things hanging from the ceiling, a cast iron skillet on the stove that looks like it has been there since before his mother was born. On the table: a plate already set. Eggs. Toast. Something green he doesn't recognize.

He is looking at the plate like it might require instructions.



Panel 2: The Grandmother enters behind him, past him, without ceremony. She rinses something at the sink. She does not perform welcome.

GRANDMOTHER: "Sit."

JENGA: "What's the—"

GRANDMOTHER: "Sit first."



Panel 3: He sits. He eats. The food is better than anything he's had in recent memory and he absolutely does not say so. He eats the green thing without asking what it is. It tastes like something that knows where it came from.

JENGA (internal): Don't ask. Just eat it.

(No sound. Just the fork. The window. The garden outside.)



Panel 4: He looks at the framed drawing on the wall across the kitchen — the one he found yesterday. His sigil. In the daylight it looks different: more deliberate. The paper is old. Someone drew this carefully, with full attention, the way he draws.

JENGA: "That drawing."

GRANDMOTHER (not turning from the sink): "What about it."

JENGA: "Who drew it."

(Beat.)

GRANDMOTHER: "Your grandfather."



Panel 5: Jenga's face. He processes this. His grandfather: another absence, another name that was never given a story. He stares at the drawing. The drawing looks back.

JENGA: "Did he know what it was?"

GRANDMOTHER (drying her hands, finally turning): "Did you?"

(She looks at him the way she looked at him across the parking lot — the same quality. The question is not rhetorical. It is patient. It has time.)

(Jenga has no answer. She doesn't wait for one. She moves toward the door.)

GRANDMOTHER: "Finish eating. Then come outside."


## PAGE 23 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: Outside. The garden at morning. The Grandmother is moving through it with purpose — harvesting something, checking something, performing the ten thousand small decisions that running a living garden requires. Jenga stands at the edge of it in his city shoes, watching.

His city shoes: white-soled, low-cut, designed for pavement. They look deeply incorrect here.

GRANDMOTHER (without looking up): "You can put those at the door."

JENGA: "My shoes?"

GRANDMOTHER: "There are boots by the door. Brown ones."

JENGA: "Are they—"

GRANDMOTHER: "They'll fit."



Panel 2: He goes back for the boots. Returns. The boots are large, worn, the leather worked soft from years of use. They fit, barely. He stands at the garden's edge again, now looking slightly more like someone who belongs here, and slightly less so simultaneously.

GRANDMOTHER: "See that area past the cedar? Where the ground is uneven."

JENGA: "Yeah."

GRANDMOTHER: "Clear it. Pull everything that's dead. Leave what's living."

JENGA: "How do I know which is which?"

(The Grandmother looks at him. The question is sincere. She respects that.)

GRANDMOTHER: "Dead things break. Living things bend."

(She goes back to her work.)



Panel 3: Jenga at the indicated area — a patch of ground beyond a cedar tree where dead growth has accumulated over a season. He kneels. Touches a dry stalk. It breaks. He touches another one — it bends. He looks at his hand like it has learned something without asking him.

CAPTION: "Dead things break. Living things bend."

CAPTION: "He thought about this longer than he expected to."



Panel 4: He works. Panel shows the passage of time — the accumulation of cleared dead growth beside him, his hands dirtier, the patch slowly opening up. He is not skilled at this. He is slow. He makes mistakes (pulls a living thing by accident, looks at it, feels bad about it, sets it aside). His city posture is wrong for this — back too straight, knees too close together, arms reaching when he should be settled.

But he works.

CAPTION: "He had no idea what time it was."

CAPTION: "He'd checked his phone four times. Still no signal."

CAPTION: "He stopped checking."


## PAGE 24 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: Mid-morning. He sits back from the clearing, work done. His hands: filthy in a new way — not spray paint dirty, not street dirty. Soil dirty. Under the nails, in the creases of his palms. He looks at them with the expression of someone encountering an unfamiliar map.

JENGA (internal): This is what dirt is.

JENGA (internal): Like. Actual dirt.



Panel 2: He looks at the cleared patch of ground. He's done reasonable work — it's not expert, but it's done. The earth is visible now. Dark, dense, much darker than he expected earth to be.

And then: something in the cleared area catches his eye.

Not dramatic. Just a noticing.

The root system of the cedar tree, partially exposed at the soil's surface where erosion has worn away the top layer — it emerges from the earth in a specific pattern. He frowns at it. Leans closer.



Panel 3: Close on the roots. The pattern is not random. Roots move through soil following moisture and resistance — they have their logic. But this pattern: the way the main root spirals outward, the secondary roots extending from it in intervals, the tertiary rootlets forming a network from those — it describes a spiral. A specific spiral. One he knows.

He has drawn this spiral since he was nine.

CAPTION: "He told himself it was coincidence."

CAPTION: "He told himself this the way you tell yourself something when you already know it isn't true."



Panel 4: He stands. Steps back. Looks at the land around him with new eyes — not the eyes of someone who has been sent to do a task, but the eyes of someone who has started to suspect the task was a context for something else. He looks at the trees in the small clearing: the way they are positioned, the space between them. He turns in a slow circle, taking it in. His body goes still in the way it goes still when he faces a wall he is about to read.

CAPTION: "He started looking."


## PAGE 25 — 3 Panels
Panel 1: He walks the perimeter of the cedar area, following the line of trees. The more he looks, the more he sees. The root systems of multiple trees, where they surface — each follows the same basic spiral logic. The distribution of trees in the small clearing — the spacing between them forms a pattern he knows. He stops. Looks again. Looks again.

JENGA (internal): Stop.

JENGA (internal): Look at it.



Panel 2: Wider shot. He is standing at a specific point in the clearing — we see what he's seeing from above: the positions of the trees around him, their root systems just visible at the soil surface, and the shape they make together. We recognize it before he names it. We have seen it twenty times in the last two chapters. The roots. The spirals. The network. It is not identical to the sigil. But it is not different from the sigil either. It is the sigil as the land expresses it. The original. The thing he has been drawing was always a copy of this.

(No caption.)



Panel 3: Close on Jenga's face. Not wonder exactly — something quieter and heavier than wonder. Recognition that has physical weight. His jaw has gone slightly slack. His eyes are doing the listening thing. His hands at his sides.

CAPTION: "He had been painting this for five years."

CAPTION: "Before he had ever seen this land."

CAPTION: "Before he knew this land existed."

CAPTION: "His hands knew something his life didn't."


## PAGE 26 — Splash
The view from directly above the cedar clearing.

Full page. Jenga at the center — small, still, standing in the boots that don't quite fit. Around him: the trees in their positions, the exposed root systems, the spacing, the whole pattern.

It is unmistakable.

The sigil. The roots, the spirals, the network. Not painted. Not drawn. Grown. The land itself carries the shape. It has always carried the shape. The land was always transmitting. His hands were always receiving.

The boy stands in the center of what he's been drawing.

The boy is standing inside his own painting.

CAPTION: "The signal had been working."

CAPTION: "He just didn't know he had been answering it."

## PAGE 27 — 5 Panels
Panel 1: He walks back to the house slowly. He is not in a hurry. Something about rushing feels wrong. He is aware of where his feet are — on this specific ground, in these specific boots, carrying what he now knows.

CAPTION: "He thought about telling her."

CAPTION: "Then he thought: she already knows."

CAPTION: "Then he thought: tell her anyway."



Panel 2: The kitchen. The Grandmother is doing something at the counter — preparing something for later, something with the dried things from the ceiling. She doesn't look up when he comes in.

JENGA: "I found something."

GRANDMOTHER: "Mm."

JENGA: "Outside. In the cedar clearing. The root systems — the way the trees are positioned—"

GRANDMOTHER: "Mm."

(She is still working. She is not performing disinterest. She is genuinely unbothered. This is not new information to her.)



Panel 3: Jenga stopping. Looking at her.

JENGA: "You know."

GRANDMOTHER (still working): "Yes."

JENGA: "You knew before I went out there."

GRANDMOTHER: "Yes."

JENGA: "Did you send me out there so I'd—"

GRANDMOTHER (finally stopping, looking at him with that quality of seeing): "I sent you out there to clear the dead growth." (beat) "What you found, you found."



Panel 4: Jenga standing with this. The distinction she's drawing — task versus discovery — is precise. She didn't manufacture the discovery. She created the conditions for it. That's a different thing. He understands it in his body before he understands it in his mind.

JENGA: "How long has it been there."

GRANDMOTHER: "The trees?"

JENGA: "The pattern."

GRANDMOTHER (returning to her work): "Always."



Panel 5: She glances at his hands — the soil under his nails, the dark earth in his palm creases. Something in her face: not quite approval, not quite satisfaction. More like: confirmation. As if his hands having soil in them is a specific milestone she has been tracking.

GRANDMOTHER: "Wash up. Lunch is almost ready."

(She says it the way someone says the most ordinary thing. Because to her, it is ordinary. This is how the day goes. This is how it has always gone.)

(Jenga looks at his hands. Looks at hers — her hands, which have been in this earth for decades. The same earth.)


## PAGE 28 — 4 Panels
Panel 1: That afternoon. Jenga in his room. Window open. The sounds of the land outside — now slightly more familiar, slightly less alien. His sketchbook open on the bed. He's been sitting with it for a while, not drawing.

CAPTION: "He had always drawn when he didn't know how to think."

CAPTION: "But he didn't know where to put this."



Panel 2: His hands over the blank page. The soil is washed off but his palms still feel different — still carry some information from the morning that his city hands didn't have before.

JENGA (internal): You've been drawing a place.

JENGA (internal): You've always been drawing a place.



Panel 3: The sketchbook. One thing discovered about the work in the cedar clearing that wasn't immediately obvious: in one corner of the cleared area, at the base of the largest cedar, there is a section of ground that someone once worked and that has grown back over. The soil in that spot is different — darker, turned over, long ago, and then left. The way you can tell a wound healed: the scar tissue is a different texture than the surrounding skin. Something was started here. Something that didn't finish. The evidence is old enough that it predates Jenga, but not by so many years that it predates everything.

He draws this from memory. Just that corner of the clearing. The tree, the base, the different soil. He doesn't know why he draws it.

(No caption. Just the drawing on the sketchbook page — and the reader, who is starting to suspect.)



Panel 4: Evening coming through the window. The light going amber and long. Jenga lying back on the bed, sketchbook on his chest, eyes open. From below: the sound of the Grandmother moving through the house. Something on the stove. The ordinary sounds of someone living.

CAPTION: "He thought about the apartment."

CAPTION: "The sketches he'd left on the wall."

CAPTION: "The city was still running its programs."

CAPTION: "Out here, something ran a different kind of program."

CAPTION: "He was starting to think he had always been part of it."


## PAGE 29 — 3 Panels
Panel 1: He opens his sketchbook to a fresh page. This time, he draws. Not the sigil — not the familiar shape. He draws the cedar clearing from memory. The specific positions of the trees. The texture of the dark soil. The quality of the morning light when he knelt there.

He draws the place.

He has never drawn from a specific place before. Every mural, every sketch — pulled from something internal. Now he draws what is outside him, and the strange thing is: it looks like his work. It looks exactly like his work.

CAPTION: "He drew the place."

CAPTION: "It looked like the inside of his mind."



Panel 2: His pen slows. He looks at what he's made — the drawing of the clearing, which contains the sigil-pattern without him having traced it intentionally. The pattern emerged from drawing truthfully. The land contains the pattern. Drawing the land truthfully returns the pattern. Inside and outside are the same language.

JENGA (internal): Oh.

(Just that.)



Panel 3: He closes the book. Lies back. Above him: the ceiling with the one diagonal crack. The moth is gone — somewhere else now, or sleeping in a way moths sleep. The sounds outside. The Grandmother below.

CAPTION: "He had painted the mural on Marcus and 9th."

CAPTION: "The mural had not been painted over."

CAPTION: "He was starting to understand what that meant."

CAPTION: "Not the legal part."

CAPTION: "The other part."


## PAGE 30 — Splash
Before full dark, he goes outside one more time.

The cedar clearing in the evening light — the trees catching the last amber from the west, the shadows long and specific. He stands at the edge of it. He doesn't go in. Just looks.

From this distance, from this angle, in this light: the pattern is less obvious than it was this morning. It's there — he knows where to look now — but you'd have to know what you were seeing to see it.

He takes out his sketchbook. Draws it from this angle too.

His hand is steadier than it's ever been.

CAPTION: "The land remembers."

CAPTION: "The signal moves in both directions."

CAPTION: "His hands had always known this."

CAPTION: "He was just now learning to trust them."
