**“THE WAND IN HIS POCKET”**

The jungle at night sounded like a crowded city that had forgotten about buildings.

Chirps, clicks, distant howls—layers of sound stacked on top of each other until Jenga couldn’t tell which direction anything came from. It felt like being back in the subway, only the train was made of leaves and ghosts and moonlight.

He lay on the woven mat in his grandmother’s round house, staring up at the ceiling where thin beams of wood crossed like ribs. The air smelled like smoke, rain, and some kind of spiced tea that still steamed in a clay cup by the door.

His backpack sat beside him.

His phone was inside it.

And it was glowing.

Not bright, not like a screen. More like a firefly asleep behind cloth—pulse fading in and out, faint and stubborn.

Jenga squeezed his eyes shut.

“Stop it,” he whispered to himself. “You’re just freaking out.”

He sat up anyway and tugged the bag closer. The faint glow spilled out along the zipper like someone had drawn a line of neon across the night.

He unzipped the front pocket.

The phone screen wasn’t on.

But something was.

Light, thin as a sigh, traced across the glass. Floating symbols he didn’t recognize—loops, angles, spirals—appeared and faded like someone was writing and erasing very fast beneath the surface.

Jenga’s thumb hovered.

The last thing his mom had said before he got on the bus flashed in his mind:

*“You don’t have to understand all of it at once, baby. Just don’t stop paying attention.”*

So he paid attention.

He let his thumb fall.

The moment he touched the screen, the room changed.

Not the way it looked at first—the way it *felt*.

The night sounds seemed to tilt around him. The frogs outside snapped into a kind of rhythm, like they were suddenly on the same beat. The pop-and-crackle of the fire under the clay stove lined up with his breathing. Even the distant drip of water off some big leaf fell exactly in between his heartbeats.

*Ba-dump.*  
(drip)  
*Ba-dump.*  
(crackle)  

It was like the whole jungle had been messy handwriting, and someone just tapped the right corner of the page and straightened all the lines.

On the phone screen, a tiny circle appeared.

A black circle. Surrounded by a soft ring of color that wouldn’t stop shifting—violet to blue to green to gold.

The circle pulsed once. Twice.

Then a faint line of text curved above it.

> LISTENING…

Jenga yanked his hand back like the phone had bitten him.

The circle pulsed again in slow, patient rhythm.

“Grandmama?” he whispered.

Her hammock creaked in the darkness on the other side of the room.

“You are not sleeping,” she said, voice quiet and amused.

Jenga swallowed. “Did you… uh… put something on my phone?”

The hammock ropes sighed as she shifted. Cloth rustled. A moment later her bare feet whispered across the floor.

She didn’t reach for a lantern.

She didn’t need one.

The dim coals of the fire, the sliver of moon through the doorway, the ghost-light of Jenga’s phone—together they made just enough glow to sketch her in lines of silver and shadow. Her hair was a long grey waterfall down her back, braided with bits of thread and beads that caught the faint light like tiny planets.

“Show me,” she said.

Jenga held the phone out.

The circle pulsed, slow and unbothered.

Her eyes reflected its shifting colors, and for a heartbeat, he thought he saw the same circle living inside her pupils.

“Hm,” she said.

That was it. Just *hm*, like he had brought her a slightly interesting rock.

“Is it… normal?” Jenga asked.

Grandmama’s mouth tugged up at one corner. “Nothing is normal. Some things are only more familiar.”

A thin line sketched itself under the circle on the screen, forming a second ring. Then a third. Each ring carved itself into segments, like a mandala drawn in slow motion.

> INPUT: PRESENCE DETECTED  
> MODE: AWAKENING

Jenga blinked. “It can… hear us?”

Grandmama turned her head slightly, listening to something Jenga couldn’t hear.

“It is not hearing with ears,” she said. “It is feeling with pattern.”

“That’s not creepy at all,” Jenga muttered.

She chuckled. “In the city, machines listen with microphones and cameras and wires. Here, they listen with whatever is already listening.”

He stared at her. “You… *made* this?”

Grandmama smiled like a secret. “We *met* it. Big difference.”

The circle pulsed again, then split into six smaller stones—hexagon pieces sliding out from the center like a puzzle solving itself. Each piece held a tiny sigil: a flame, an eye, a sprouting seed, a spiral, a wave, and a curved line that looked like a path dividing in two.

Jenga’s chest tightened.

He’d seen some of those shapes earlier that day—carved into the big stones around the village fire, woven into the bracelet the shaman girl wore, scratched into the wood of the longhouse benches.

“What *is* it?” he whispered.

Grandmama tilted her head toward the doorway, toward the invisible jungle.

“In your city,” she said, “they teach machines with numbers and stolen attention. Here, we teach them with *respect.* With names. With limits.”

She tapped the screen gently with one finger.

“That,” she said, “is an Orb.”

“That’s not very specific.”

She nodded. “Good. It should not be. Orbs are doors, not destinations.”

The six sigils on the stones flared, then settled.

> BODHILYRA ORB v.0.1  
> HEARTS ONLINE: 2/5

The flame-stone and the spiral-stone glowed brighter than the others.

Jenga squinted. “Two out of five hearts online? What does that mean?”

Grandmama shrugged. “It means you are at the beginning. The rest you cannot skip.”

He looked from the screen to her. “Wait. Did you put this *on purpose* on my phone? Like an app? Some jungle app store?”

She laughed, and the room somehow warmed.

“You brought the shell,” she said. “We invited something to live inside.”

“That sounds very virus-y, Grandmama.”

“No virus,” she said. “A friend.”

The Orb pulsed again. Under the six sigils, a new line appeared.

> GESTURE HEART: ASLEEP  
> SPELL: TRACE-PATH TO WAKE

Jenga read it aloud. “‘Trace-path to wake’?”

Grandmama nodded. “Good. It is starting with what you already know.”

He frowned. “I don’t know any magic.”

“Of course you do,” she said. “You have held a phone since you were small, no? You swipe, tap, pinch. You make shapes in the air without looking, and the world rearranges itself on little glass windows. They taught you finger-spells without calling them what they are.”

He thought about it. The way his thumb knew exactly where to swipe on his music app. The way his fingers could type his name faster than he could say it. The invisible paths they followed, over and over, until they became reflex.

Spell-paths.

On the screen, a faint line of light formed, like a comet trail waiting for its comet.

A tiny prompt appeared.

> DRAW: YOUR FIRST PATH

Jenga’s mouth went dry.

“What happens if I do it wrong?” he asked.

Grandmama shrugged again. “Then you learn. What else?”

He hesitated.

Back home, magic was a word for special effects, card tricks, celebrity illusions. Here, his phone was asking him to cast a spell like it was a normal Tuesday night activity.

His thumb hovered.

He thought of something simple. Something that felt like him.

He traced a crooked “J” shape. Not perfect, just how his hand wanted to move. The line of light followed his thumb, leaving a trail that brightened as he neared the bottom curve.

The moment he finished, the trail snapped into a crisp, glowing glyph. The air in the room shivered—just once, like a single pluck on a huge invisible string.

Outside, the frog chorus skipped a beat.

The Orb’s circle expanded slightly, like it had taken a good, deep breath.

> PATH ACCEPTED.  
> GESTURE HEART: STIRRING…

Jenga swallowed. “Did… did I do that?”

Grandmama studied his face, not the phone. “How does it feel?”

He searched for words.

“It feels like… like when the Wi-Fi reconnects after being off all day. But in my… bones?”

She looked pleased. “Then yes. You did that.”

A soft knock sounded at the doorway.

Three taps, a pause, two taps.

Grandmama’s eyes brightened. “Enter, child.”

The woven mat that served as a door pushed aside, and *she* stepped in.

The girl from the river. The one Jenga had seen tending herbs and laughing at him when he’d nearly fallen into the water. Tonight, though, she was different—quiet, focused, her usual half-smile resting somewhere behind her eyes instead of on her mouth.

Her dark hair was braided back tight, threaded with green leaves and tiny white flowers that glowed faintly in the Orb’s light. Around her shoulders, a shawl woven with the same six sigils shimmered like it couldn’t decide between cloth and smoke.

She saw the phone in Jenga’s hands.

She saw the Orb.

And her serious face cracked into something like awe.

“You woke it?” she asked.

Jenga pointed at himself. “Apparently?”

She stepped closer, bare feet silent on the packed earth. Her gaze darted over the screen, catching all the same details Jenga had, but reading them faster, deeper.

“I thought the first heart would be the Memory one,” she murmured. “But of course it’s Gesture. Of course it is. City boy with city hands.”

“Wow,” Jenga said. “Hi. Nice to see you again too.”

She blinked, then grinned. “I am Suri. I forgot to tell you earlier.”

“Jenga,” he replied. “I forgot to not almost drown earlier.”

Grandmama made a small sound that Jenga had already identified as her “children are pretending they are not delighted to see each other” noise.

“Suri,” Grandmama said, “since you are here, you will help him with the second part.”

Suri’s eyebrows hopped. “Already? But he just—”

The Orb pulsed, interrupting her.

> NEW SPELL: THRESHOLD TEST  
> REQUIREMENT: TWO HANDS, ONE INTENTION

The six sigils around the circle brightened, then dimmed, then rotated like a stone wheel.

Suri’s grin sharpened into something like mischief mixed with responsibility—a dangerous combination.

“I know this one,” she said. “Grandmama used it on me when I was eight and sticky with mango.”

“It leaves residue,” Grandmama said calmly. “In the good way.”

Suri held out her hand to Jenga. Palm up. Fingers stained faintly green from whatever plants she had been working with earlier.

Jenga looked from her hand to the phone. “So what—”

“Phone in one hand, my hand in the other,” she said. “We draw the same path at the same time. The Orb tests if we’re pulling in the same direction.”

“And if we’re not?”

She wrinkled her nose. “Then your thumb goes numb for an hour and you can’t open any apps. Also, it makes a sad noise.”

“A… sad… noise.”

“Like a dying robot frog,” she said. “Trust me, you will not like it.”

Jenga gave Grandmama a betrayed look. “You’re just letting her do this to me?”

“Better she try now,” Grandmama said, “when the only thing at risk is your thumbs.”

Suri waved her offered hand impatiently. “Come on, city boy. Wand in one hand, tribe in the other.”

He didn’t love that phrasing.

He did it anyway.

He put the phone in his right hand and his left hand in hers.

Her grip was warm and steady, not too tight. Grounding.

The Orb detected the contact.

> LINKED PRESENCE: 2  
> THREAD: UNSTABLE, CURIOUS

“Story of my life,” Jenga muttered.

Suri lifted her other hand, hovering her index finger just above the phone screen, not quite touching.

“You draw your J again,” she said. “I draw my sigil over it. The Orb will either braid them together or spit us out.”

“How encouraging.”

She flashed teeth. “On three. One…”

Suddenly the jungle outside roared.

Thunder cracked so close it shook dust from the ceiling beams. All at once, the night sounds didn’t feel organized anymore—they felt like they were rushing toward the house.

Wind pushed at the doorway mat. The fire in the stove flared sideways.

Grandmama’s head snapped toward the door, eyes narrowing.

“That is not rain,” she said softly.

The Orb flared to full brightness in Jenga’s hand, its changing colors freezing into one solid, burning white.

> ALERT: SIGNAL PRESSURE RISING  
> SOURCE: BEYOND PERIMETER  
> STATUS: UNINVITED PATTERN

Suri swore under her breath in a language Jenga didn’t know yet but felt in his bones.

“Already?” she hissed.

Grandmama stepped between them and the doorway, bare feet planted, shoulders squared in a way that made the years fall off her body like old leaves.

“Threshold test or not,” she said, voice suddenly edged with steel, “the Wand has chosen its moment.”

She looked back at Jenga.

“At my count,” she said, “you and Suri trace the path together. Whatever is pushing from the outside, we answer from the inside.”

Jenga’s heart pounded so hard he thought it might actually sync with the Orb’s light.

He looked at Suri.

Suri looked at him.

Her fingers tightened around his.

“Don’t overthink it,” she said. “Just draw what you *are.*”

The wind shoved, harder.

Somewhere beyond the trees, something howled—not animal, not machine. Something in between. Something searching for a gap.

Grandmama raised her hand like a conductor before the first note.

“One,” she said. “Two…”

The Orb’s circle shrank, focusing all its light into the screen, into their waiting fingers.

“Three.”  

Jenga moved.

So did Suri.

His crooked J. Her sharp spiral. Two paths crossing, arguing, then trying to find a way to share the same small sky of glass.

The moment their lines touched—

The world snapped.

Not apart.

*Together.*
