## CHAPTER FOUR — **THE HUM OF THE ROOTS**

The jungle is never quiet.  
It hums.

Not loudly. Not in words.  
A vibration beneath insects, beneath leaves, beneath breath.

Jenga notices it first at dawn.

He wakes before the birds. The air is thick and blue. The fire from the night before has burned itself into a perfect circle of ash. When he steps into it barefoot, the hum sharpens—like a bass note finding its key.

Panels here are **wide and slow**.  
Soft gradients. Fog drifting between trunks. Subtle motion lines around roots.

Jenga places his hand on the ground.

The earth answers.

Not with sound—but with **memory**.

Fragments flicker:  
– hands planting seeds  
– feet dancing in rain  
– a child laughing, then crying, then laughing again  

He pulls back, shaken.

That’s when **Amaru** appears.

She doesn’t arrive like a hero.  
She’s already there—sitting cross-legged on a fallen log, braiding a vine into her hair like it’s always been that way.

She watches him, smiling.

“You felt it,” she says.

Jenga doesn’t answer. He can’t yet.

She explains gently:  
The jungle speaks in *patterns*.  
Roots remember.  
Stones store rhythm.  
People forget—but can relearn.

Amaru is training to become a **keeper of resonance**—someone who listens for imbalance and tunes it back into harmony. Not a healer in the soft sense. Not a warrior either.

A tuner.

She warns him:  
Not everyone who listens hears the same thing.  
Some hear power.  
Some hear fear.

The hum deepens.

Final panel:  
A massive root system beneath the forest, glowing faintly—like a sleeping circuit.

## CHAPTER FIVE — **THE MASKS WE WEAR**

The village prepares for the **Night of Many Faces**.

This chapter is visually playful, slightly surreal.  
Masks hang from trees. Lanterns sway. Shadows dance wrong.

Each mask represents a role:  
Hunter. Mother. Trickster. Guardian. Witness.

Jenga chooses none.

Amaru notices.

“You still think you’re just passing through,” she says.

He snaps back:  
“I didn’t choose to be here.”

She nods.  
“No one ever does.”

During the festival, something shifts.

The drums change tempo.  
The hum becomes erratic.

A figure appears wearing a mask no one recognizes—smooth, dark, reflective. It mirrors whoever looks at it.

This is **The Hollow Guest**.  
Not a villain in the cape-and-fire sense.  
A *void where meaning slips*.

Children feel dizzy near it.  
Fires dim.  
The ground loses rhythm.

Jenga, without thinking, steps forward.

He doesn’t fight.  
He *removes his mask*—revealing his bare, confused, human face.

The Hollow Guest cracks.

Because it feeds on performance.  
On pretending.  
On dissonance.

Amaru joins him. She removes hers too.

Others follow.

The Guest dissolves—not defeated, but **understood**.

Afterward, Jenga realizes something terrifying and beautiful:

He didn’t act because he knew what to do.  
He acted because he *felt aligned*.

Final panels:  
Masks hanging quietly at rest.  
The jungle hum stabilizing—lower, warmer.

## CHAPTER SIX — **THE SILENT ECHO**

This chapter is quieter.  
Fewer words.  
Longer pauses between panels.

Jenga walks alone to a stone clearing marked with symbols older than language. This is a **Listening Place**.

Amaru watches from afar. She knows this step must be taken alone.

He sits.

Nothing happens.

Then—memories surface.

Not his.

Echoes of others who sat there:
– a woman who left the jungle and returned decades later  
– a boy who stayed and became a guide  
– someone who failed, learned, and tried again  

No prophecy.  
No destiny stamp.

Just **possibility**.

Jenga understands the core truth of SacredSpace:

You are not here to escape the world.  
You are here to learn how to *enter it without losing yourself*.

The hum fades…  
…and becomes something subtler.

A **silent echo** that follows him even when he stands.

Final panel:  
Jenga walking back toward the village.  
Not claimed.  
Not owned.  
But **listening**.

# CHAPTER VII — **THE LATTICE SONG**
The jungle hum is steady again.

Too steady.

Jenga notices it when the birds stop improvising.

Their calls repeat in exact intervals.  
Not wrong—just… *too perfect.*

He walks with Amaru along a path that wasn’t there yesterday.

The vines are pulled aside like someone combed them.  
The mud is packed flat like a road.

Amaru’s smile is gone.

> “Someone tuned the forest,” she says.  
> “But not by listening.”

They reach a clearing where the trees stand in an unnaturally even circle.  
The roots—normally wild—form a clean geometric lattice beneath the surface.

In the center sits a stone, newly polished, reflecting the sky like a mirror.

Jenga feels his thoughts speed up near it.

Plans appear in his head fully formed:
- how to “master” the hum  
- how to control the village  
- how to become important  
- how to never feel lost again  

The stone doesn’t speak.

It **promises**.

Amaru steps between Jenga and the stone.

> “It offers certainty,” she says.  
> “Certainty is the bait.”

Jenga’s chest tightens.

> “What’s wrong with certainty?”

Amaru looks at him like she’s choosing her words carefully so they don’t become a spell.

> “Certainty is a cage that feels like a throne.”

The hum changes.

A new rhythm overlays the jungle—straight, crisp, repetitive.

**The Lattice Song.**

The trees begin to sway in sync, like puppets.

Jenga sees it then:  
the lattice isn’t just in the roots.

It’s in people.

A villager approaches—eyes bright, smiling too wide.

> “We found a better way,” the villager says.  
> “No more confusion. No more pain. The forest will be perfect.”

Amaru’s voice drops:

> “Perfect is what the Hollow wears when it wants your soul.”

Jenga’s hands shake.

He wants to touch the stone.

He wants the plan.

He wants to stop feeling like a stranger inside his own life.

Amaru places a hand on his shoulder.

Not forceful.  
Not pleading.

Just present.

Her touch does something the stone can’t:

It doesn’t offer certainty.

It offers **company**.

Jenga breathes.

The silence inside his lungs pushes back against the Lattice Song.

For one beat—one sacred beat—the jungle returns to messy, living rhythm.

The villager’s smile falters.

The polished stone cracks—not shattered—just… **imperfect**.

And in that imperfection, the real hum leaks through.

**Final Panel:**  
A hairline fracture across the mirror-stone.  
Inside the crack: soft green glow, like roots reclaiming memory.

Caption:  
**THE FIRST WEAPON IS BREATH.**
