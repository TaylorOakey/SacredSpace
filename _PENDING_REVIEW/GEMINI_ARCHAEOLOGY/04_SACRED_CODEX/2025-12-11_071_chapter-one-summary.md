# **CH∆PT3R 0N3 — *The City That Forgot to Listen***

Jenga always said the city had **two heartbeats**.

One belonged to the people—fast, jittery, buzzing like a mosquito against a window. The other was quieter. Older. A slow pulse under the concrete that only he seemed to notice. It thumped at the edge of hearing, somewhere between the subway rails and the spaces where broken bricks met the earth.

He didn’t tell anyone about the second heartbeat anymore. Adults in the city didn’t like mysteries they couldn’t invoice or pave over.

But Jenga heard it that morning as clearly as sirens.

He stood on the balcony outside his tiny eighth-floor apartment, backpack dangling off one shoulder, eyelids half-glued with sleep. Below him the world churned: buses coughing smoke, vendors shouting at ghosts, pigeons strategizing petty crimes.

Then the **deep thump** rose from beneath the streets.

One beat…  
Pause…  
Another…  
Like something sleeping under the asphalt had rolled over and exhaled.

Jenga felt the air thicken, a honey-slow shimmer that nudged his awareness sideways. That shimmer had a sigil in it—one he’d seen before only in sketches, those shapes his hands drew without asking permission. A looping line, a spiral hook, a crossbar that hummed at the edges.

He blinked. The sigil vanished.

His phone buzzed in his pocket. A text from his mom.

**DON’T MISS THE BUS TODAY. Love you. And listen to Grandma.**

Jenga sighed. The “don’t miss the bus” part meant she was working double shifts again. The “listen to Grandma” part meant she was serious.

He didn’t know yet that this would be his last morning in the city for a long, long time.

Downstairs the bus wheezed to a stop. Jenga slipped inside, kept his hood low, and found a seat near the back. The bus smelled like warm plastic and old stories.

He was halfway to school when the **sigil appeared again**, traced in fog on the window beside him. Nobody else reacted. The driver hummed a tune. Kids argued over headphones.

Jenga lifted his finger and traced the faint glyph.

The fog lit for a moment—gently, as if approving his touch.

A soft click sounded in his mind. Like a lock surrendering its last bolt.

Then a voice whispered, not in his ear but in that odd space just behind thought:

**“Awaken, little one. The First Path has opened.”**

Jenga jerked upright. The bus lurched. No one else seemed to hear anything.

A woman in the next seat glanced at him. “You okay, kid?”

He nodded, but he wasn’t okay at all. Something ancient had just leaned close. Something that smelled like moss after rain and sounded like a drum carved from the ribs of a storm.

When the bus reached his stop, he stepped off into a morning that no longer felt like it belonged to him.

At school, everything was normal—too normal. Teachers droning. Screens flickering. Friends laughing in loud circles. Jenga walked through the day as if wearing someone else’s body.

At 2:54 p.m. his name echoed over the crackling intercom.

“Jenga Oakely. Please report to the front office. Bring your things.”

A few kids oohed. Someone whispered, “What’d you do?”

He didn’t answer. His chest tingled—the way it tingles right before lightning strikes the tallest tree.

In the office, his mother waited with her coat half-zipped and eyes too bright.

“Hey, sweetheart.” Her voice tried to sound casual and failed. “Change of plans.”

Behind her stood her mother—**Grandma**, the medicine woman of a tribe Jenga didn’t know well enough to claim but felt tethered to anyway. Grandma’s cane was wrapped in painted runes. Her eyes shimmered like they held campfire reflections only she could see.

“Pack light,” she told him. “The jungle has sent for you.”

Jenga swallowed. “The jungle?”

Grandma tilted her head, amused. “You’ve been hearing it, haven’t you? The second heartbeat.”

The room spun.

“How did you—” Jenga began.

“Because it called me first,” she said. “And because the sigil on your window this morning was the **First Stone**. Only a child chosen by the forest spirits draws it without knowing.”

Jenga’s mom squeezed his shoulder, her expression soft and apologetic. “You’re going to live with Grandma for a while. It’ll be good for you. And… she says it’s important.”

Important. The word carried weight, but not the heavy kind. More like anticipation bending the air.

Jenga nodded slowly.

Outside, as they walked toward the car, he heard it again: the deep pulse under the world.

One beat.  
Pause.  
Another.

Grandma looked at him. “That’s not the city, little one. That’s **the Door**.”

Jenga clutched his backpack. “What door?”

She smiled, gentle and terrifying. “The one you were born to open.”

The sigil appeared again—this time on the sidewalk, traced in shadows by no hand he could see.

He realized something then: this wasn’t the beginning of a journey.

This was the moment his life remembered itself.

And somewhere far from the city, in a jungle he had never seen, a girl training to become a healer paused mid-breath… as if she too felt the First Stone stir.
